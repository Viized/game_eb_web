Hunt, Carole. “Worn Clothes and Textiles as Archives of Memory.”

Critical Studies in Fashion & Beauty, vol. 5, no. 2, 2014, pp. 207–232. doi:10.1386/csfb.5.2.207_1.

This is by far the most and the only essay that connects most of the scholars and materials I searched on, including: Matter and Time from Bergson, Benjamin, the concept of "thing" and "thingness" from Heidegger, materials/ materiality/ material culture from Daniel Miller, A Poetics of Space from Bachelard, and many. Concepts such as mnemonic, involuntary memory, and archivization are also discussed. Responding to the way I want the game to be structured, it also analyzes how materials, in this case textiles, can not only help to generate memories and keep through traces, which may enable memory stimulation and sensory reexperiences differing from individual to individual across the boudaries of space and the limitation of time, but also assist in reconstructing and recollecting with the unavoidable risk/products of mixing and creating with the haunting of the present, whether we want or even realize them or not.

In the abstract and introduction, Hunt starts with our common misunderstanding of how we normally frame materials, working purely as portals for meanings greater than and beyond them, while neglecting the fact that materials themselves are actively involved in the sensemaking process, and their "thingness" even when working as tools for artistic expression. (Howes and Attfield) "...material culture offers an understanding of how people make sense of the world through physical objects."Then it goes on talking about why and how one could generate more based on already existing memories, using Woolf's writings related to seemingly unreasonable and questionable details of the involuntary memory of the past. I wasn't quite realizing how deeply I manipulate my past and dreams until I started to document them because of my current work and journals. (Bal) "...as an activity occurring in the present, in which the past is continuously modified and redescribed." I used to held a very negative and passive feeling about this, but since tertiary retention and Stiegler, right now, I am more convinced that (especially during the age of digital), we might win a chance to take advantage of this if we constantly reflect on the instability through self-driven recollecting of the memory and actively making new connections between stuffs. (Stiegler, resist against the lose of individuality) (Stop accepting external and passive systems of categorization like core aesthetics.) Susan Stewart: On Longing, "Micrographia".

In section B Application, starting with Derrida's archivization and archive fever, it explains how archivization is "consigning, inscribing a trace in some external location, a space outside." The concept reminds me of the different stages of memory from Stiegler, and the essence of tertiary retention, which is what/how/why to externalize memories. Part of my decision to choose the website and users' own journals as a format of creation is also related to my interest in how craft can not only be the medium of archive but the archive itself. (it's drawing, wearables, sounds, fake documentary/video clips...)

B2. Themes of the 'Gigantic', I mainly focus on the discussion of the capability of clothes to represent not only the signs and history of wearing, standing as evidence of the past and the proof of people's existence, but more importantly, they can become a site. In the second set of the game, as I plan to put the semi-open closet that holds all of my father's clothes and talk about my feelings on the gradually faded smell of him on his clothes in the whole attic room environment, my craft work as places for reliving and reflecting rather than just representing the experiences. (wonders about the narrative and text lines in the game) Meanwhile, making is a way to "rediscovered and remembered rather than forgotten." (pixelated/ blurry art style at this section)

B3. Themes of the 'Miniature' sort of validate parts of my intention, why I need my physical work to be portable, and why I want the whole display space in the future to be a smaller, more intimate room. (Bachelard, "the home as an 'archive', and for them to be accessible, they have to be small, not only for the purpose of representing intimacy through visually pleasing size.) Benjamin, "the homely is at the same time 'unheimlich'"... backing the setting of mimicking the plot of The Room, using waking up on bed as the starts of each sets and scenes. Rooms filled with everyday objects and stuff are also stages that allow plots and intense performances to happen...

In the conclusion, Hunt once again addresses the importance of textiles working as THE material, and how they differ from others by nature. How about digital, if I also regard digital game-making as a way of crafting? Thinking more about the uniqueness of each physical material and how that may shift if it happens in digital? Are the digital merely the visual re-presentation of the actual materials, or do they exist by themselves? To indicate different materials visually by changing the setting in the shader or procedural nodes, are they different or the same?

Krzywinska, Tanya, et al. “Coziness in Games: Second Homes, Audiences and Esthetics.”

Game Studies: The International Journal of Computer Game Research, vol. 20, no. 3, Dec. 2020, gamestudies.org/2003/ articles/krzywinska .

This article discusses the concept of coziness and how coziness has been manifested in game playing. By incorporating elements of walking simulators and cozy gameplay experiences into my project, I gain a deeper understanding of what coziness entails, which will ultimately enhance my analysis of Animal Crossing.

Before reading, I normally presume that cozy games need to be associated with certain portrayed themes and/or interactive in-game items, and cozy leans more towards an aesthetic or artistic visual expression. However, this mindset pre-sets the audiences and players as passive acceptors of the games and their narrative, in contrast to the player-text approach. Games should not only be limited to and understood as the process of accepting information, following presumptions, and reacting to designed rules.

According to the article, cozy games are not "casual playing", and rather than understanding it as a genre itself, "cozy" is more described as a gaming experience that often involves individuals' perceptions based on their living historical differences and ways that players build up between cozy games and themselves; therefore it is crucial to keep in mind that the perception of coziness is always unavoidablly subjective. To better define cozy, the article firstly mentions the connections between cozy, home and the feeling of home "as a place to live and feel secure" (4). Then it argues about how coziness needs to be acknowledged as "structural components" (4) with three main aspects being fulfilled: safety, abundance and softness. I'm also very interested in the aspect of cozy games working as a way of self-care, mentioned at the bottom of page six. I assume that part of my reason why I originally did not intend to do a narrative-based story-driven game is that I would rather provide experiences and a playable scene to interact with, instead of a game that has a clear and linear storyline. (I am thinking of adding a scene where players can drag objects in the previous three and create their own new scene, or choose one from the three to decorate with objects across scenes in the way they want, and I am curious about how people from different cultural backgrounds would understand these spaces through interactions.)

What comes next in the articles is to analyze deeper about where and how the coziness comes from through three sections: game aesthetics and mechanics, coziness in non-cozy games, the home space and communities. The second part is more inspiring for me on how to conceptually structure the game-playing flow and artistically arrange spaces and elements for various rooms along the idea of 'predictability' and 'familiarity/reliability'. Meanwhile, games as a second home and "an open possibility space" for experiencing and experimenting with "imaginative engagement" is also something I'm curious to explore more about, drawing examples across different genres, specifically horrors, considering personally how frequently I found coziness overlaps with them. (Waszkiewicz, Soft Horrors: The visual and ludic safety of dark cozy games)

Taylor, Nicholas. The Grounds of Gaming. Indiana University Press, December 2024.

In chapter 2, Circle: It all starts at home, Taylor analyzes what and how players set up games and their related hardware in the domestic spaces. From the history of how equipment were advertised and markets straight towards males, and the hidden messages of designing experiences based on the needs of men, the chapter begins by referring back to the history of gameset as practices of masculinity in domestic place making. Even with examples like Wii, which is targeted at family-friendly consoles (similar to Switch right now, in actual daily use, it is hard for consoles to actually encourage playing as a family unit, taking myself as an example). Then the chapter goes to the collective data based on the images of the game setup at home, based on discussion from forums on NeoGAF posts, and starts to categorize the setup of different levels of immersive gaming experiences into three types in general: Hearth, Bunker, and Cabinet. In my case, I am interested in the cabinet the most, as the book suggests, they are reflections of ourselves and our relationship with the surroundings and other family members as well (also may explain why I fancy the wunderkammer/the cabinets of curiosity so much in the first place, and the aesthethics simply brought by making the arragement of the "gears" in the environment).

In the living room from my scene 3, I find that my living space is usually semi-open, fulfilling my need to stay isolated and somehow open simultaneously. I would not consider them as setting stages for reality escape initially, without pointing out by the book, and looking back right now, it is also interesting to see how I modified the attic rooms, then had to move back to the living room because I still expected some communications with my mother while playing.

Compared to the closet, my home home during primary school, shown as down left corner beside the balcony in the room on the top left in set 3, the setup made by my father and used by the family was more bunker-type, (re-read 94, Bunkers versus Feminism), where I played the begining plots repeatedly of games like Silent Hill: The Room, House of the Dead 2?, Return to the Castle Wolfenstein, and many online easy-playing games.
