Hunt, Carole. “Worn Clothes and Textiles as Archives of Memory.”

Critical Studies in Fashion & Beauty, vol. 5, no. 2, 2014, pp. 207–232. doi:10.1386/csfb.5.2.207_1.

This essay uniquely connects various scholars and materials I have researched, including Bergson’s “Matter and Time,” Benjamin’s insights, Heidegger’s concepts of “thing” and “thingness,” Daniel Miller’s discussions on materials, materiality, and material culture, and Bachelard’s “A Poetics of Space,” among others. It also delves into concepts such as mnemonic, involuntary memory, and archivization. In relation to my vision for game structure, the essay analyzes how materials, particularly textiles, can generate and preserve memories through traces. These elements may enable memory stimulation and sensory re-experiences that vary from person to person, transcending spatial boundaries and the constraints of time. Additionally, it explores how they assist in reconstructing and recollecting memories, albeit with the unavoidable risks and products of mixing and creating—often accompanied by the haunting presence of the present, whether we consciously acknowledge it or not.

In the abstract and introduction, Hunt begins by addressing our common misunderstanding of how we typically frame materials, viewing them solely as portals for meanings greater than themselves. This perspective neglects the fact that materials are actively involved in the sense-making process and possess their own “thingness,” even when serving as tools for artistic expression (Howes and Attfield). “…material culture offers an understanding of how people make sense of the world through physical objects.” The discussion then shifts to exploring how one could generate more from already existing memories, drawing on Woolf’s writings related to seemingly unreasonable and questionable aspects of involuntary memory from the past. I wasn’t fully aware of how deeply I manipulate my past and dreams until I began documenting them due to my current work and journals. (Bal) “…as an activity occurring in the present, in which the past is continuously modified and redescribed.” I used to hold a very negative and passive view of this, but since engaging with tertiary retention and Stiegler, I am now more convinced that, particularly in the digital age, we may have the opportunity to leverage this process if we continually reflect on the instability of our memories and actively forge new connections between them. (Stiegler, resisting the loss of individuality.) We should stop accepting external and passive systems of categorization, such as core aesthetics. As Susan Stewart discusses in “On Longing,” the concept of “Micrographia” also plays a significant role in this exploration.

In Section B, “Application,” the discussion begins with Derrida’s concept of archivization and archive fever, explaining how archivization involves “consigning, inscribing a trace in some external location, a space outside.” This concept resonates with the different stages of memory as outlined by Stiegler, particularly the essence of tertiary retention, which focuses on the what, how, and why of externalizing memories. Part of my decision to choose the website and users’ journals as a creation format stems from my interest in how craft can serve not only as a medium for archiving but also as the archive itself. This includes various forms such as drawings, wearables, sounds, and fake documentary or video clips.

In the section titled ‘B2. Themes of the Gigantic,’ I primarily focus on the discussion of clothing’s capability to represent not only the signs and history of wear but also to serve as evidence of the past and proof of people’s existence. More importantly, clothes can become a site of meaning. In the second set of the game, I plan to include a semi-open closet that holds all of my father’s clothes, where I will express my feelings about the gradually faded scent of him lingering in the attic room environment. My craft work aims to serve as places for reliving and reflecting on experiences rather than merely representing them. This raises intriguing questions about the narrative and text lines within the game. Additionally, the act of making becomes a means to be ‘rediscovered and remembered rather than forgotten.’ I envision this section featuring a pixelated, blurry art style to enhance this theme.

In B3, “Themes of the ‘Miniature,’” I explore aspects that validate my intention behind creating portable physical works and my desire for the entire display space to be a smaller, more intimate room. This idea is informed by Bachelard’s notion of the home as an ‘archive,’ suggesting that for these works to be accessible, they must be small—not only to represent intimacy through a visually pleasing size. Benjamin’s concept that “the homely is at the same time ‘unheimlich’” further supports this setting, as it aligns with my aim to mimic the plot of The Room, beginning each set and scene with the act of waking up in bed. Rooms filled with everyday objects serve as stages that allow for plots and intense performances to unfold.

In the conclusion, Hunt once again emphasizes the significance of textiles as a unique material, highlighting how they inherently differ from others. This prompts me to consider the realm of digital media: if I regard digital game-making as a form of crafting, how does this perspective change? What happens to the uniqueness of each physical material when translated into the digital space? Are digital representations merely visual re-presentations of actual materials, or do they exist independently in their own right? When we indicate different materials visually by altering settings in shaders or procedural nodes, are they fundamentally different, or do they retain the essence of their physical counterparts?

Krzywinska, Tanya, et al. “Coziness in Games: Second Homes, Audiences and Esthetics.”

Game Studies: The International Journal of Computer Game Research, vol. 20, no. 3, Dec. 2020, gamestudies.org/2003/ articles/krzywinska .

This article explores the concept of coziness and its manifestation in gameplay. By integrating elements of walking simulators and cozy gameplay experiences into my project, I gain a deeper understanding of what coziness truly entails. This understanding will ultimately enhance my analysis of Animal Crossing.

Before reading, I typically assumed that cozy games needed to be associated with specific themes and/or interactive in-game items, viewing coziness more as an aesthetic or artistic visual expression. However, this mindset positions audiences and players as passive acceptors of the games and their narratives, contrasting with the player-text approach. Games should not be limited to merely accepting information, following preconceived notions, and reacting to designed rules.

According to the article, cozy games are distinct from “casual playing.” Instead of being defined as a genre, “cozy” is better understood as a gaming experience shaped by individuals’ perceptions, influenced by their unique historical contexts and the relationships players build with cozy games. Therefore, it is crucial to remember that the perception of coziness is inevitably subjective.

To better define cozy, the article first explores the connections between cozy, home, and the feeling of home as “a place to live and feel secure” (4). It then argues that coziness should be acknowledged as comprising “structural components” (4), fulfilled by three main aspects: safety, abundance, and softness.

I am particularly interested in the idea of cozy games functioning as a form of self-care, mentioned at the bottom of page six. I believe part of my initial hesitation to create a narrative-based, story-driven game stems from my desire to provide experiences and interactive scenes rather than a game with a clear and linear storyline. I am considering adding a scene where players can drag objects from the previous three environments to create their own new scene or choose one from the three to decorate with objects across different scenes as they wish. I am curious to see how people from various cultural backgrounds would interpret these spaces through their interactions.

The articles then delve deeper into the origins of coziness through three sections: game aesthetics and mechanics, coziness in non-cozy games, and the home space and communities. The second part is particularly inspiring for me as it offers insights on how to conceptually structure the game-playing flow and artistically arrange spaces and elements for various rooms, centred around the ideas of ‘predictability’ and ‘familiarity/reliability.’

Additionally, the notion of games serving as a second home and as “an open possibility space” for experiencing and experimenting with “imaginative engagement” is something I am eager to explore further. I aim to draw examples from various genres, specifically horror games, as I have personally observed how often coziness overlaps with them. (Waszkiewicz, “Soft Horrors: The Visual and Ludic Safety of Dark Cozy Games”)

Taylor, Nicholas. The Grounds of Gaming. Indiana University Press, December 2024.

In Chapter 2, “Circle: It all starts at home,” Taylor analyzes how players set up games and their related hardware in domestic spaces. The chapter discusses the historical advertising of equipment targeted towards males and the hidden messages that shape gaming experiences based on men’s needs. It begins by referencing the history of game setups as practices of masculinity in creating domestic spaces. While consoles like the Wii are marketed as family-friendly (similar to the Switch), in practice, they often fail to encourage family play, as I’ve experienced personally. The chapter then examines collective data from NeoGAF forum discussions, categorizing home gaming setups into three general types: Hearth, Bunker, and Cabinet. I am particularly interested in the Cabinet type, as the book suggests these setups reflect our relationships with our surroundings and family members. This might also explain my fascination with wunderkammer or cabinets of curiosity, as they aesthetically enhance the arrangement of “gears” in our environment.

In my living room from Scene 3, I find that my space is usually semi-open, allowing me to feel both isolated and connected at the same time. Initially, I wouldn’t have considered it a setting for escaping reality, but reflecting on the book’s insights, I see how I modified the attic rooms for that purpose. Ultimately, I had to return to the living room because I still wanted to maintain some communication with my mother while playing.

Compared to the closet setup in my home during primary school, which is depicted in the bottom left corner beside the balcony in the top left room of Set 3, the setup created by my father for family use was more of a bunker type (as discussed on page 94, in “Bunkers versus Feminism”). In that space, I repeatedly played the initial plots of games like “Silent Hill: The Room,” “House of the Dead 2,” “Return to Castle Wolfenstein,” and various online casual games. I also find it interesting to compare this setup with my cousin’s gaming gear in his living room during the same period.

Flanagan, Mary. Critical Play: Radical Game Design. MIT Press, 2009. Chapter 2, “Playing House,” pp. 29–66.

Flanagan’s Playing House chapter examines how domestic play has long been used to rework cultural paradigms, especially through experimenting with artificial identities, self-expressive environments, and humorous scenarios (17). She emphasizes that “childhood itself is a cultural invention” (25), and that toys like dollhouses acted as a “faithful mirror of what happened in real homes,” revealing how play and daily life have been intertwined for more than a century (30).

What stands out to me is her framing of play as a form of “rewriting” (33), where domestic roles and narratives are not simply repeated but reimagined. This directly connects to my own work, since I am not only archiving but also reshaping memories and spaces through crafted environments. Flanagan’s references, such as Rosler’s Semiotics of the Kitchen (48), show how even everyday domestic labor can be transformed into a performative critique, which resonates with my blending of memory work and digital staging.

Finally, her attention to “domestic places being represented in digital games” (49) gives me a strong anchor: the home is both a literal space of play and a symbolic one. It pushes me to think of my game sets—closets, attics, living rooms—not only as reconstructed environments, but as playful, critical sites where cultural roles and personal memory collide.

Flanagan, Mary. “Reskinning the Everyday.” Space Time Play: Computer Games, Architecture and Urbanism: The Next Level, edited by Friedrich von Borries, Steffen P. Walz, and Matthias Böttger, Birkhäuser, 2007, pp. 308–315.

Flanagan’s essay Reskinning the Everyday calls attention to how we engage with interfaces in ways that are often invisible to us. She observes that “while designers and engineers work to erase mechanical portals between users and machines, and science fiction writers fantasize about direct mental connections to computing systems, it seems the opportune moment to shift ourselves away from our perception of current, everyday computing experiences” (305). This resonates with my own reflection on how easily I naturalize daily interactions with screens and inputs, rarely questioning their cultural or political weight.

She admits, “in the daily quest to transfer ideas from head to document, I often take these interfaces for granted” (307), and here I’m reminded of Thomas Kuhn’s work on paradigms: just as scientific frameworks structure what can be thought, the metaphors embedded in our technologies constrain how we imagine and act. Flanagan points out the irony that “Internet browsers rarely show users how data gets from point A to point B, as though the structure of the system has nothing to do with its content” (312). This makes me think about my own game environments as not just containers but structured, biased systems shaping meaning.

By “mapping ubiquity,” she argues that “the blurring of space, location, and time leaves no sense of near or far, past or present,” while reminding us that “metaphors in programming have a strong affect on how digital tools are made and used” (315). This deeply connects to my exploration of tertiary retention: the way metaphors, architectures, and even invisible protocols inform what kinds of memories can be externalized, stored, and replayed. For me, the essay encourages a more critical practice—treating both material craft and digital craft not just as surfaces, but as systems of mediation and rewriting.

Kerich, Chris. “Polygonal Modeling: The Aestheticization of Identity.” Proceedings of the 2019 DiGRA International Conference: Game, Play and the Emerging Ludo‑Mix, DiGRA, 2019, Tampere, Finland. DiGRA Digital Library, https://doi.org/10.26503/ dl.v2019i1.1070.

Kerich explores how the process of polygonal modeling, central to 3D graphics and game design, transforms identity into an aestheticized construct. The argument begins with how modeling reduces bodies into vertices, edges, and polygons — an abstraction that is not only technical but also cultural. This abstraction encodes ideas of efficiency, smoothness, and “recognizable” identity, which in turn shape how digital bodies are produced and perceived.

Kerich emphasizes that modeling is never neutral. The very structure of polygons frames identity as modular, editable, and reproducible. In this way, modeling echoes what Thomas Kuhn describes about paradigms: once a system of representation is in place, it not only organizes practice but also limits what can be imagined within it. The “aestheticization” emerges from this process — identity is no longer lived, but represented as a visual surface, optimized and rationalized for legibility.

At the same time, Kerich points to the contradictions in this system. The push toward realism in polygonal graphics often conceals the instability of identity, reducing it to stylized tropes or standardized bodies. Yet the very tools of modeling can also be sites of resistance: by exposing the “constructedness” of polygons, artists and players might destabilize these conventions and open possibilities for critical play.

For me, this resonates with Benjamin’s notion of reproducibility and Stiegler’s tertiary retention. Polygonal modeling externalizes identity much like recording externalizes memory — turning the self into manipulable data. It also connects back to my own project: when I craft avatars or environments, I am working within a system that both enables expression and enforces constraints, asking whether identities in digital spaces are ultimately fluid or flattened into aesthetic codes.
